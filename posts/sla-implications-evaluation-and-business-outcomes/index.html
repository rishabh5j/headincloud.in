Service Level Agreement(SLA) : Implications, evaluation & business outcomes

Rishabh Jain
 2022-05-19  2022-05-19  496 words   3 minutes 



When we talk about designing highly available applications or architecture on public cloud, SLA is one of the single most important component to determine the service availability. It’s calculation and outcome directly influences the business and customer-service provider trust. Let’s give some time understanding it better.

Service Level Agreement is a commitment or agreement between customer and the service provider(of sorts) which calculates the standard of service being offered. Failing to meet these standards would mean a financial reimbursement or service credits being incurred on the service-provider.

Choosing the right SLO : Before we dig deeper, it’s more important to understand what Service Level Objectives(SLO) are. SLOs are the pre-determined metrics that are used to define availability and reliability of service. Let’s take an example. Company A is a e-commerce organization, which hosts it’s API gateway on public cloud provider B. Any failures to entertain the new order requests via API directly translates to revenue loss. It makes sense for the customer A and provider B to accept requests processed per hour as the agreed SLO. Let’s skew the requirements a bit more and assume A is a trading platform to facilitating stock market trading. In such a scenario, request processing latency is of paramount importance, and qualifies for SLO as well. Since SLOs are the defining metrics for SLA, collecting these values on a reqular interval at pre-defined rate is quintessential.

Calculating the SLA : Selection of the right SLO is half a battle won. Now let’s look at the SLA calculation. Company A anticipates around 10k new orders via API calls. Senior management in Company A decides: failure to accept more than 100 requests per year lead to breach of trust with their end consumers and look real bad on their reputation. That would mean 0.1% failure per year, or 99.9% calculated SLA. From service availability standpoint, a 99.9% SLA would mean service is down for ~9 hours in a year.

More is better? : But why not expect a higher SLA from service provider? More is good, right? Wrong. With each increasing 9, the availability time improves drastically (for instance 99.99% availability would mean only 10 new order requests failing in a year, out of 10K in the context of example aforementioned) but it does introduces a lot of complexity in the architecture and subsequently increases the cost — both deployment and operational. Increasing availability requirement mandates each contributing service or component to provide equal reliability, else it puts the entire solution at the risk to meet the commited SLA. For instance, higher SLA requirement from a API interface would mean choosing carefully the backend messaging queue and the database instance as well, and provide necessary redundancy wherever required.

Hence, it is upto the business to draw a fine line between expected level of reliability AND growing complexity to attain higher reliability. Keeping unrealistic SLA target tends to be counter-productive and may lead to tightly coupled applications.
